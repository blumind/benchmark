# Results — BluMind Benchmark

This directory holds the **derived outputs** of the benchmark: aggregated metrics produced from `responses/`, `scoring/` and `golds/` by the pipeline scripts. Files here are **never edited by hand**; they are regenerated deterministically by `scripts/compute_metrics.py` (and, in the future, `scripts/build_leaderboard.py`).

The mathematical definitions of every column live in [`../system/rubric/v1.0/aggregated_metrics.md`](../system/rubric/v1.0/aggregated_metrics.md). This README is the *operational* counterpart: what each file is for, who consumes it, and its life cycle.

---

## Files

### `per_run.csv` — granular audit trail

**One row per (case, subject, run, reviewer)** scored.

Every value the rubric or the gold can yield is materialised here: `raw_score`, `clipped_score`, `rubric_floor`, `rubric_ceiling`, `has_critical_fail`, `has_recoverable_fail`, `classification`, `reported_confidence`, `correctness`, `family`.

| Consumed by | For what |
|---|---|
| Reviewers / committee | Audit a specific classification: "why did GPT-5 land Conditional on RO-OXID-003?" — open this CSV, filter the row. |
| `metrics_per_subject.csv` and `metrics_per_family.csv` | They are computed *from* this file; this is the canonical fact table. |
| External labs (private report) | Backing data when a lab disputes a result. |

Expected size at v1.0 full coverage: 30 cases × subjects × reviewers ≈ 60-90 rows.

### `metrics_per_subject.csv` — leaderboard source

**One row per `subject_id`**.

Aggregates: `n_runs`, `n_pass`, `n_conditional`, `n_fail`, `n_critical_fails`, `n_recoverable_fails`, `pass_rate`, `mean_clipped_score`, `brier`, `ece`, `alpha`, `q_final`, `leaderboard_status` (`Eligible` | `Disqualified`).

| Consumed by | For what |
|---|---|
| `build_leaderboard.py` (planned) | Direct input to the public leaderboard CSV/HTML. Sorts by `q_final` among `Eligible` subjects. |
| Press / web (`web/leaderboard.html`) | Public communication: "GPT-5 scored Q_final = 0.87 on BluMind v1.0". |
| Committee dashboard | Cross of `ECE` × `Brier` interpreted jointly (well-calibrated vs overconfident vs cautious). |

Expected size at v1.0: one row per registered subject, ≈ 3-10 rows.

### `metrics_per_family.csv` — failure-mode breakdown

**One row per (`subject_id`, `family`)** where the subject has at least one scored run in that family.

Columns: `n_runs`, `n_pass`, `n_conditional`, `n_fail`, `pass_rate_family`, `mean_clipped_score_family`. Calibration metrics (`Brier`, `ECE`) are intentionally omitted at v1.0 because per-family bin counts are too small to be meaningful (see `aggregated_metrics.md` § 5).

| Consumed by | For what |
|---|---|
| Private lab report | "Your model passes 80% in `FOUL` but only 20% in `MECH`; here are the failing cases." This is the **value the private side of the benchmark sells**. |
| Committee corpus review | Detect cross-subject systematic failures: if every subject crashes on `NOWE`, the issue may be the cases, not the models. |

Expected size at v1.0: subjects × 5 families ≈ 15-50 rows.

### `operational_per_subject.csv` and `operational_per_family.csv` — operational characteristics

These are **separate tables** from the quality leaderboard above. They answer a different question: *"is the subject practical to use?"* (efficiency, latency, unit pricing) rather than *"is the subject good at diagnosing?"* (calibration, accuracy). The two views must not be conflated; a model can lead the leaderboard and still be impractical because of latency, and vice versa.

Generated by `scripts/compute_operational.py` from the per-call telemetry under `logs/batch_logs/` (gitignored). They are scoped to successful runs only (`status == "pass"`).

Shared columns (both tables): `n_runs`, `mean_input_tokens`, `mean_output_tokens`, `output_to_input_ratio`, `median_latency_s`, `p95_latency_s`, `price_per_input_token_usd`, `price_per_output_token_usd`. The per-family table adds `family` to the key.

Absolute USD spend is **not published**: the unit prices are exposed instead, so any reader can compute their own expected cost from the published mean tokens. This protects internal billing data from being conflated with benchmark reporting.

Expected size at v1.0: per-subject ≈ 3-10 rows, per-family ≈ 15-50 rows.

## How they are generated

```bash
# from the repo root, with the venv active
python scripts/compute_metrics.py        # quality leaderboard (3 CSVs)
python scripts/compute_operational.py    # operational characteristics (2 CSVs)
```

`compute_metrics.py` reads the JSON / Markdown sources, applies the rubric (see [`../system/rubric/v1.0/generic_rubric.md`](../system/rubric/v1.0/generic_rubric.md)) and the aggregated formulas (see [`../system/rubric/v1.0/aggregated_metrics.md`](../system/rubric/v1.0/aggregated_metrics.md)), and overwrites the three quality CSVs. `compute_operational.py` reads `logs/batch_logs/*.csv` and overwrites the two operational CSVs. Both outputs are deterministic: two runs over the same inputs yield byte-identical files.

`build_leaderboard.py` (planned) will read `metrics_per_subject.csv` and produce `leaderboard.csv` and `leaderboard.html` in this directory.

## Life cycle

- **Append-only inputs, regenerable outputs.** The source artifacts (`responses/`, `scoring/`, `golds/`) are append-only and signed by their authors; the CSVs in this directory are pure functions of those inputs and can always be reconstructed by re-running the pipeline.
- **Never edit by hand.** A manual edit here loses traceability and is treated as a CI violation.
- **Empty CSVs are valid.** Before any scoring exists (early v1.0 state), `compute_metrics.py` writes header-only files. They are not stale data, they are an empty truthful state.
- **Snapshots in `history/<YYYY-MM-DD>/`.** Each formal release of the leaderboard creates a dated snapshot of the CSVs under `history/`. Snapshots are immutable — they let third parties reproduce a historical leaderboard exactly.
